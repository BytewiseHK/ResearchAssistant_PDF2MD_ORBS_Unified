# Fascism as Ideology

We can understand fascism by thinking about how it generally differs from other right-wing movements.

– Commonality: right-wing – Differences: different emphases

# Left-Wing

Communism or Socialism

Attempt to bring about a new society – through revolution

# Right-Wing

Main right-wing alternatives to socialism:

– Reactionary: reacts against attempts to change the status quo   
– Reformist: wants to change the status quo, but tries to do so through reforming it; gradual changes over time

– Fascism

# 3 Core Themes of Fascism

(1) The “New Man” (2) Strong Nationalism (3) “Third Way” state

# The “New Man”

This is a new type of leadership.

• Rejection of the old “establishment” elites, because they are “corrupt”.

• Replace them with a new elite group of leaders.

The new elites are “real people,” and they are stronger, so they are better leaders.

Violence

# Violence

• Violence is important for bonding among people in society.

• Violence creates martyrs, and this inspires the people to give up comfortable lives.

• Violence also prepares people for war, which is necessary in international relations.

# Strong Nationalism

• The focus can be on preserving culture or on racism. – These are tendencies but can go together.

• Violence is relevant for nationalism also. – Often, violence was used to protect “culture.” – One common way this was done was to prevent colonial peoples from gaining national consciousness/identity.

# “Third Way” State

• The third way is supposed to be between capitalism and socialism.

– Using state planning in the private market to ensure the market achieved the goals of the state.

# Fascism

Focus on the need for new elites.

These elites truly represent the people.

The people are united (as a nation) based on ideological conceptions of identity (cultural or biological, or both).

Rejecting capitalism and socialism.

# Justice and Ideology
